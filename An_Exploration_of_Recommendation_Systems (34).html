  1. Introduction

    What people consume (sometimes literally) is directly influenced by computer-generated suggestions. From finding love to finding breakup songs, multi-billion dollar companies utilise recommendation systems (RSs) to drive user engagement and ad revenue. This essay will delve into the mechanics, challenges, and potential improvements of recommendation systems, while briefly touching on the ethical implications surrounding certain uses.

    So, what are recommendation systems? If I were talking to a 5-year-old I’d explain it like so:

    Recommendation systems are like friendly robots that suggest things you might like based on what you have liked before.

    If you are above the age of 5, first of all, congratulations, and secondly, let’s go a step further. A RS generally consists of 3 parts [1]:

    1. Collecting data about user behaviour, such as search queries, purchase history, or viewing habits.

    2. Analysing the collected data using algorithms or machine learning techniques to gauge user preferences and interests.

    3. Use these interests to recommend items that they are likely to enjoy based on their past behaviour.

    This technology has found widespread use across various industries: it’s the driving force behind Amazon’s Things You Might Like Section, Spotify’s Daily Mixes, and Instagram’s Explore Page. These are the go-to, intuitive examples but there are more intriguing applications. For instance, they can help determine a person’s desired travel destinations, food preferences on food delivery apps, and even identify what kind of people a person may be interested in for online dating. The (arguably sneaky) influence RSs have on our day-to-day lives can’t be understated. We’ll touch more on this later when discussing echo chambers, addiction, and targeted advertising but for now, let’s consider why RSs are so beneficial.

    “Getting information off the internet is like taking a drink from a fire hydrant.” - Mitchell Kapor

    Before utilising effective information filtering, online services were bloated with content resulting in users facing information overload. Additionally, consumers could only rely on personal recommendations from a small circle of friends and family [2]. Recommendation systems, sometimes known as “information filtering systems”, aim to resolve these issues, and ultimately give companies a better understanding of their customers. Let’s explore the two major paradigms [3].


  2. What Changed?

    1. Content-based filtering (CBF)

      Content-based filtering involves analyzing the attributes of items, or their “content”, to suggest other items that are similar to those that the user has previously liked [4]. A user may “like” an item by engaging with it for an extended period of time, making a purchase, or visiting it repeatedly. Conversely, they may “dislike” an item if they skip past it quickly or return a product. Additionally, users may explicitly give their preferences on items (or certain types of items) through rating systems, or a like/dislike system. CBF stands out for its speed and scalability, as it does not rely on other users’ information and therefore requires less memory. Additionally, it can capture a user’s niche interests that only appeal to a small audience. However, this feature is symptomatic of a broader challenge with content-based filtering: it relies on pre-existing interests to make new recommendations leading to a limited capacity to expand user interests. Furthermore, the attributes of items are hand-selected (to an extent) which can be a limiting factor in the quality of recommendations as it is difficult to account for every

      feature.


    2. Collaborative filtering (CF)

      So how can collaborative filtering address the issues in CBF? David Goldberg, who coined the term “Collaborative Filtering” with Tapestry (a mail filtering system) in 1992, believed that incorporating other humans into the filtering process would improve these systems [5]. This approach uses similarities between users in order to generate new recommendations [6].

      A simple music streaming application is an ideal use case for collaborative filtering as it can quickly exhaust the number of quantifiable attributes available for analysis. For example, two songs may have a similar key, bpm, and genre, but one could be Adele pouring her heart out while the other is a Kidz Bop cover of Adele pouring her heart out. In reality, streaming services will likely use a hybrid of the CF and CBF which we will explore in the next section.

      Another benefit is that users can now discover new interests - if someone similar to you likes something, you will probably like it too. In Tapestry’s case, users were able to annotate documents, which allowed for filtering based on personalized preferences using customized queries like “Retrieve all emails related to recommendation systems that Rosie enjoyed”.

  3. What Now?

    Starting with e-commerce, industries started to realise the business value of good recommendations. In the late 1990s, Amazon launched their collaborative filtering method, successfully increasing their sales. Computing power increased, and companies began to invest a significant amount of time and resources into research in the field, which ultimately led to the development and adoption of hybrid RSs.

    Essentially a catch-all term, hybrid RSs are any filtering system that utilises aspects from both CF and CBF so that their shortcomings counteract one another [7]. It can also involve incorporating other strategies like heuristics, weightings, or machine learning to enhance their performance and quality. By leveraging these additional techniques, hybrid RSs surpass the capabilities of pure CF/CBF [8]. It is the norm for each company to implement their own proprietary hybrid recommendation system(s). Let’s use Netflix as a case study for the current state of RSs when used in industry.

    It utilises CF by comparing your preferences to those of similar users, and CBF by using item-specific information such as the year and genre of a show or movie to guide new recommendations. Additionally, the system takes into account contextual factors such as the time of day and how recently you watched a certain show to fine-tune its suggestions. Unique to Netflix, they have staff that watch films and assign them microtags as metadata. This is then used to automatically categorise TV shows and films into over 70,000 microgenres: “Gory Foreign Satanic Stories” and “Understated Romantic Road Trip Movies” to name two. These microgenres can be used to make even more niche, user-specific recommendations. To stress the importance of a good RS, 80% of TV shows people watch on Netflix are discovered through its recommendation system [9].


  4. What the Future Might Look Like

Social media platforms featuring short-form content, such as TikTok and Instagram use RSs to produce an endless stream of personalized entertainment. However, this can result in addiction and other negative mental health outcomes, such as low self-esteem [10][11]. Given that a majority of users are young, it remains unclear what the long-term impacts will be.

Filter bubbles occur on social media when a users feed becomes increasingly homogenous, which can be directly caused by the RS. This can have two negative consequences: it inhibits a user’s ability to explore new interests, and, more insidiously, it rejects outside opinion and only reinforces pre-existing biases leading to potentially harmful effects on the user’s perception of reality [12]. Political campaigns have exploited filter bubbles by using targeted ads or fake accounts to manipulate public opinion, creating division and threatening democracy [13]. To combat this, social media companies and governments must cooperate to promote transparency, and media literacy, and lawmakers must regulate political advertising.

Finally, The privacy risks involved with gathering and processing data are often overlooked. [14] The vast quantity of user data, makes them a tempting target for potential cyber-attacks. Consequently, there have been calls for stronger legislation to regulate the gathering and storage of this data to better protect user privacy and security.

We are heading in a direction of more data, more computation, and more intelligent means of analysis (through AI) and there’s no doubt that these systems will improve. Additionally, the areas in which RSs will apply continue to expand. As an example, in the future, RSs may be used to suggest appropriate physiotherapy routines [15]. Legislation must continue to try and keep up with the influence modern RSs hold, and ensure that they are used in a way that is beneficial to the general population.
